About — for policymakers

This site is built for ministry staff, civil servants, and policy analysts in Hungary who need quick, credible evidence for real decisions.

What you can get from it

  • Evidence on a specific question. Search for a topic ("minimum wage", "foreign-currency mortgages", "Roma education") and find 3–5 peer-reviewed papers with plain-language summaries, the data behind them, and a policy-relevance note.
  • An expert to consult. Every paper links to its authors' websites and institutional pages, where you can find their contact details. If a paper is relevant to a decision you're making, the author is almost always willing to talk.
  • Comparative context. Summaries explicitly name the country studied and flag external-validity caveats, so you know what translates to Hungary and what doesn't.

How to use it

  1. Start from the topic or the search bar. The 15 policy topics on the landing page give the broad map; the search bar on every page does full-text search across titles, abstracts, summaries, and authors.
  2. On a paper page, read the "Policy relevance" section first. It's short and bulleted — one screen is enough to decide whether the paper is worth going deeper on.
  3. Check the data description and the summary next. If the policy claim looks useful, look at what data (which country, which years, what sample) and what method produced it — that's how you judge how credible the result is and how well it transfers to Hungary.
  4. Follow up if needed. Follow the DOI link to the published paper; email the author if you have a follow-up question.

Talking to a researcher

Hungarian economists are, in general, very willing to talk to policymakers. A short email to the corresponding author stating "I'm at the Ministry of X, working on Y, and your paper is potentially relevant" will almost always get a response. There is no formal process.

If you want an introduction or don't know whom to ask, write to the editor (bekesg@ceu.edu) with a short description of your question — I'll point you to the right person.

How the non-technical summaries are made

Each paper on the site has three short, policy-facing fields: a plain-language summary, a description of the data used, and a note on policy relevance. These are drafted with AI help from the abstract and (where available) the introduction and conclusion — and reviewed by a human editor before publication. Labels on each paper indicate review status: ai-drafted / human-reviewed / author-approved.

Who is included

Economists admitted to the catalogue have at least one publication in a top general-interest or top field journal — roughly the top 50 economics journals plus top finance and management. See the full list on the journals page. "Hungarian" is interpreted broadly: Hungarian nationality, born in Hungary, or three or more years at a Hungarian institution. Diaspora is explicitly included.

Limitations you should know about

  • This is a beta. Many summaries are first-draft AI output — clearly labelled. Trust the paper's DOI link more than the summary.
  • Author selection was mostly AI-driven with light editorial review. Suggestions for missing names are very welcome.
  • Some papers have Hungarian translations of the summary, some don't (yet). Missing translations fall back to English with a note.
